Chlorine Bleach Vs. Hydrogen Peroxide

Luke Priest
Published 1/14/25

Why Should You Care?

In recent years there has been worries about potential danger that arises from the use of chlorine based bleach. So today we will be taking a look Hydrogen Peroxide, a potential alternative to think about.

What Is Chlorine Bleach?

For our fellow chemistry-minded individuals, it may be helpful to know what these chemicals actually are. Chlorine based bleaches active ingredient is Sodium Hypo-chloride (NOCl). In most household cleaning cases this chemical is diluted to about 5%. [1]

What Is Hydrogen Peroxide?

Hydrogen Peroxide (H202) has often been used to treat wounds. In most household uses this chemical is also diluted to around 1-5%. [2, 3]

Making The Healthy Choice

As you may have heard, never mix bleach with other chemicals, doing so can create toxic gas! Even by itself it can cause irritation, cell death, and if consumed can cause extreme organ damage. [4, 5]

Though not as known for mixing products it is also a poor idea to mix anything with Hydrogen Peroxide. In low concentrations as home use has if consumed it can cause mild irritation and can lead to vomiting and diarrhea. It may even cause airbubbles in your bloodstream if you ingest it! [6, 7]

Choice For The Earth

Chlorine based bleaches being as harmful it is to humans, it can be far more dangerous for smaller wildlife and plants. If flushed down the drain there is a large risk of mixing with other chemicals such as drain cleaners and create toxic gas. [1, 7, 5]

Hydrogen Peroxide being less harmful to humans it is also less harmful to smaller wildlife, and plants. [5] Though, it is still very important to take care to use hydrogen peroxide carefully. Going down the drain is still a risk aswell. Hydrogen Peroxide also breaks down into the environment as water and oxygen. [6, 8]

Effectiveness of These Disinfectants

Bleach and Hydrogen Peroxide have both been found to have fantastic properties to fight and kill bacteria and viruses such as the COVID-19 virus. [9, 1] Though Hydrogen Peroxide can lose effectiveness in high temperatures.
